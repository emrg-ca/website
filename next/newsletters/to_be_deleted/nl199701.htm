INTRODUCTION
I hope everyone had a good holiday season and is now ready to settle in to enjoy the winter. The activities of EMRG have been slow in the past few months, mostly due to my work commitments.

In December, I had the opportunity to speak to the Ottawa Amateur Radio Club (OARC) about EMRG. It is important that all local amateurs know about EMRG, even if they don’t plan to join.

I have been in contact with the RMOC regarding the role of EMRG in an emergency. This news letter will talk about this and the emergency plans that guide us.

On a sad note, December ended with amateurs assisting in a search in the Meech lake area. This has been making me think about what is required to help ensure we have amateur volunteers who can survive and be useful in all types of emergencies.

I would like you to think about EMRG and some of the roles it could play in Ottawa-Carleton.

EMERGENCY PLANS
There are many plans in place for dealing with an emergency in Ottawa-Carleton. The decision on which plan(s) to use depends on the nature of the emergency and which agencies are involved.

For the Regional Municipality of Ottawa-Carleton, the main plan is the Regional Emergency Plan.

“This plan has been formulated to assign responsibilities and to guide the actions of key officials after the onset of an emergency.”

The Regional Emergency Plan is 70 pages long and provides information on the following:
 > When, Why & How the plan can be activated
 > Key groups and positions along with their responsibilities.
 > Names and phone numbers of representatives for the key groups and positions
    outlined on the plan
 > Listing of other related plans which provide greater detail in specific areas

Is EMRG In The Plan?
EMRG shows up in two spots in the present Regional Emergency Plan. These two items are one page each and cover the broad responsibilities and expectations of the two primary EMRG contacts: the Communications Officer (Now called Team Leader) and the Field Representative.

The following are quotes taken from the Regional Emergency Plan

What is an Emergency?
Emergencies are defined as situations abnormally affecting the lives and/or property of our society which, by their nature or magnitude, require a coordinated response by a number of agencies, both governmental and private under the authority of the appropriate elected official, as distinct from routine operations carried out by agencies as normal day-to-day procedures, e.g. fire fighting, police activities, normal hospital routine.

When is the Regional Emergency Plan Activated?
 > At the request of an area municipality
 > When two or more municipalities are affected by an emergency
 > At the discretion of the Regional Chair when Regional resources are consumed in
    such a manner as to place a strain on these resources
 > When Regional facilities are threatened

EMRG’S ROLE IN AN EMERGENCY
One of the key tasks for EMRG is to plan and train, in order to be ready for an emergency. This raises the following question:

     What does the Regional Municipality of Ottawa-Carleton (RMOC) Emergency
     Measures Unit (EMU) expect of EMRG?

I spoke with Harold Murphy, the EMU manager about this. The two key points are:

The EMU looks to EMRG as a flexible resource which can provide communications from anywhere in the Regional Municipality of Ottawa-Carleton.

EMRG would be required when existing communications systems cannot handle the load or if the existing communications systems should fail.

There are no specific designated sites in which EMRG would be expected to operate, other than the Emergency Operations Centre (EOC) which is located at the RMOC building.

EMRG’S NEXT STEP
EMRG can play an important role in shaping the future emergency planning in Ottawa-Carleton.

While the RMOC does not have specific sites designated for operation by EMRG, there is a need for EMRG to identify any locations within the Ottawa Carleton Region that can benefit from improvements in communications capabilities. The 2m radio at the Kanata fire hall, provides an example of an infrastructure that can be put in place to provide points of operation that are local to an area around the region.

BEYOND THE RMOC
The Emergency Measures Radio Group can do more than sit and wait for the Emergency Measures Unit to call. There are other groups within Ottawa-Carleton that can use the resources of radio operators.

EMRG should be the focal point for any organization that requires the assistance of radio operators for an Emergency. These organizations may be within the Ottawa-Carleton area or could be neighboring radio groups needing additional resources.
