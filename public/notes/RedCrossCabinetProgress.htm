EMRG Red Cross Radio Updates

The Red Cross radio cabinet is the worst example I have of not meeting deliverables for a client, but I am making progress and the end is in sight. In 2003 the Red Cross moved from the old Plymouth St offices to their new building on Catherine St. In 2004, we began the project to install radios at the Catherine St offices, which included installation of antennas on the roof and building a cabinet in which to install the radios. The Red Cross paid for the materials that were purchased, EMRG was able to donate some parts, such as coax cable, antenna mast, TNC, power supply and a VHF radio. Through a RAC ARES grant and some additional Red Cross funding, 3 Alinco radios (144, 220, 440) were purchased for the radio cabinet.

Work progressed slowly, starting with a detailed design for the equipment cabinet to fit in the 12 in wide space available. The objective for all EMRG projects is to complete documentation that can be used to maintain what was created, or to allow anyone to duplicate what was done. As you can see, a lot of effort went into the initial design.

(NEW)
Completing the Red Cross radio cabinet is critical for several reasons;
  1. We need to progress our relationship with the Red Cross and the cabinet is both a stumbling block for discussing more interaction (we haven't completed what we started, so how can we do more), and we need the radios to communicate.
  2. The RC cabinet is the first full implementation of the EMRG Standard Radio Interface. So far the SRI is some small unit testing and a great idea, but this will put the SRI into a real working environment.
  3. The EMRG plan to provide multiple radio channels in a single site, is to use different bands and a multi band antenna. This allows the use of 144, 220 and 440, providing 3 radio channels, with one antenna and hopefully no cross interference. So far, only the EMRG communications room at Dispatch has multi-band capability and it uses different antennas, so the Red Cross will be our first site to validate that the single antenna with 3 radios will work.
  4. EMRG needs an exercise and proving out the Red Cross cabinet with 3 channels operating at full capacity in parallel will provide an opportunity to involve all EMRG members, learn about a client site, test the 3 radios in parallel, while practicing message handling.

The construction of the cabinet was completed several years ago and work began on the radio wiring. This turned out to be a project that didn't work well as a group effort at Randall, so I brought the cabinet back home where I could work on it when I had time. Time passed, but not much happened. I did complete the wiring diagram for the EMRG Standard Radio Interface (SRI) for the Alinco radios and Mike Kelly took away a bag of parts and returned the completed adapters.

I am now putting time aside to complete the work and here are some pictures of the completed DC Wiring to prove progress. The wiring is now all labelled, tested and includes the AC powerbar, power supply and the battery  with battery charger. When the system is OFF, the battery is kept up by the battery charger, which is an intelligent charger that maintains the battery at peak efficiency.

(NEW) The patch panel is mounted to its shelf, and work has begun on wiring it up. The patch panel is in layers, starting with the DC wiring and LAN HUB on the bottom, then the LAN RJ45 patch panel and RS232 connectors, then the DIN plugs an finally the DA15 connectors for the EMRG SRI (Standard Radio Interface). If you haven't heard about the EMRG SRI before, check out document EMRG-210 Standard Radio Interface.

Next step is to complete the patch panel which involves the RS232, as well as the SRI connectors. After that comes the User interface (Speaker, mic on the desk), then the radios go in and get tested. Once all that is completed and the bugs chased out, the cabinet will go to the Red Cross for installation.
